May 1, 2026 Day 1

What I worked on

Added the work I did in my holidays, before reading the project instructions, to GitHub. This work included:

  1. Reading the LivWell article and documentation and thinking of a Research Question
  2. Reading previous literature to identify gaps, common research strategies, and understanding which questions would make sense
  3. Loading the LivWell csv into Python and selecting the variables relevant to my analysis.
  4. Understanding the data: How exactly are the variables defined? And which are available?
  5. Adjusting my analysis question and model given the available data

Problems encountered

This stage of the project was riddled with issues, including the following:

  1. Operationalising the variables I thought of previously required more reasoning and theoretical argumentation than I had thought. E.g., to operationalise 'violence towards women', there were variables in the dataset that focused only on violence by a partner, or by anyone. To operationalise heat, I could have chosen average temperature, or heatwaves outside of 1SD, 2SD of local averages, etc... The dataset is large, with many variables, and that made things more interesting, but also required more reasoning.
  2. How to operationalise concepts that weren't directly in the dataset? For example, 'empowerment'.
  3. I had expected data availability from 40+ countries over 10+ years. However, the specific DV module had only been added to the surveys later on. It was also difficult to get women to share this data, so data was very sparse.
  4. Because of the sparse data, the initial panel style analysis I had planned was impossible.
  5. Additionally, I had to figure out how to plot country-based data to spot country-specific differences.

What I learned

Both coding and substantial things:

  1. To operationalise the variables, I learned to deeply understand a dataset by combining the documentation, an understanding of how the data was collected, and my own plots. Before this, I had often focused on either how researchers collected data in the real world, OR on my own plots, and rarely ever have I deeply read descriptions of individual variables. But for LivWell, I had to be able to imagine how the researchers collected the data in all these countries, how this data was gathered into regions, what exactly each datapoint was (percentage, deviation from local vs. global average, etc.), and then combine that with my understanding of the literature and what I wanted to model. Basically, it was the first time I had to use both substantial and statistical understanding in this way. That was fun and rewarding!
  2. I had to read about different ways to combine observations from different scales and of different, clustered variables, to create scores that reflect a concept and don't overweigh certain variables -> e.g. those measured on larger scales, or represented using more different measurements. I already knew some of these things, but had to remember this knowledge and add to it. I will probably decide on either PCA or a weighted score.
  3. To work around the limited data availability, I had to understand the confounds specific to climate and culture related research across regions. For example, I larned what bad control was. I also needed to refresh my understanding of fixed effects. I learned to write them down as models and imagine what would happen, algebraically, with specific datapoints, to really understand what my models did with the data and what they modelled.
  4. Next to that, I learned to think creatively about methods. So far, I have only been confronted with data that was perfectly aligned with the methods I understood - because it had been provided by a university, not by the world. To deal with real-world scarcity and still get answers out of my data, I thought of my own variation of what is basically a binomial test, close to Kendall's tau.
  5. To visualise the country-specific data, I also learned geopandas, which is a really cool tool and I hope I will use it a lot in this project.
  6. Finally, to handle the references for my literature, I learned that you can use Zotero to directly create a bibliography in html, which is perfect for this project. Isn't that amazing?

May 2, 2026 Day 2

What I worked on

Did the assumption checks for model 2, and ran it

  1. Conducted Model 2 Assumption Checks:
  2. Looked at old statistics books, remembered what assumptions i needed to check for an FE regression, googled, asked AI. E.g.: Normality of Errors, and homoscedasticity, VIF, Cook’s Distance, and clustered standard errors.
  3. Found the best library that included all tests i wanted to do: Statsmodels. Read lots of documentation and stackoverflow about it.
  4. Conducted the checks and made diagnostic plots: Q-Q plots, Residuals vs. Fitted scatters, Histograms...
  5. Analyzed the output to determine if assumptions were met. Made notes when there were issues (like heteroscedasticity) for later reporting.
  6. Finally ran the test, wrote down a first interpretation of results, tried to link the coefficients to real-world impacts based on previous knowledge I had.

Problems encountered

This stage of the project was riddled with issues, including the following:

  1. The library I used had many, but not all features I needed. For example, there was no fixed effects argument in statsmodels. So, I used manual dummy-variable encoding.
  2. Similarly, Conley standard errors seem to be pretty standard in R, but I couldn't find them for Python.
  3. Rank deficiency warnings. I did not fully understand these, and decided to ask about them in the tutorial.
  4. I got a 'ghost window' that kept opening while I was plotting, with nothing in it. It disappeared when I added plt.close() after every showing.
  5. I need variance for the analysis, but this led to the exclusion of zero-variance countries. Maybe, this is an issue with sample selection; systematically removing climatically stable regions.

What I learned

Both coding and substantial things:

  1. How to use statsmodels: how to do assumption checks and an FE regression in Python
  2. Identification is a difficult thing. Because of the limited data availability and the issue of country- and year- confounds, I had to be very careful and remove a lot of variation. There are many comparisons I couldn't make, and it made it impossible to look at what I had actually wanted to look at (differences across many years). Because I couldn't compare different countries to each other.
  3. I learned to independently find and use the documentation of Python packages efficiently, combined with online forums.
  4. I've never worked with a dataset with this many variables. But because there are so many, similar, variables, I feel like I can really observe the following while I am working: Data is far from neutral or "true". I want to look at domestic violence and heat, but it is my choice what domestic violence indicator I choose, whether I look at regions or countries, what SD I select as cutoff for my temperatures, etc. Operationalisations are also modelling. I knew this, but Livwell is really illustrating it in a way that lets me understand what it means in practice.
  5. To remember and apply insights from previous courses in my own work (which is very rewarding :) )

May 4, 2026 Day 4

What I worked on

Working out the discrepancy between my binary test and the FE model.

  1. Wrote down the different explanations for the results I could think of
  2. Thought about which of these are testable
  3. Implemented those tests I thought were possible
  4. Noted down first interpretations and conclusions

Problems encountered

The biggest issue was the danger of essentially p-hacking or justifying a null-result in accordance with my biases, after my results didn't match each other, or previous literature. But I still needed to interpret my results and think about assumptions. This was especially problematic because I had failed to think about my assumptions and the power of the test BEFORE testing.

  1. Breaking ratio data down into binary data loses a LOT of variation. With an effect size that is known to be small based on previous literature, this needs a LOT of observations to make up for the loss of power. I should have considered that.
  2. If the binary and FE test had aligned, they would have compensated for each other's weaknesses. But they didn't.

What I learned

Above all: to check my assumptions and power before doing my tests.

  1. Also: to carefully think of explanations while being aware of the dangers of post-hoc justifications
  2. P-hacking is a real risk, even if unintended
  3. Aggregation can destroy real effects, the scale at which you analyse really matters
  4. Even in large, impressive datasets, most of the data can be useless for the question at hand
  5. Turning hypotheses / potential explanations into code
  6. Coding a power analysis in python
  7. scipy.stats.binom
