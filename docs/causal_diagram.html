Pathways in the Literature

How Heat Waves Can Increase Domestic Violence Mechanisms

1. Biological and Neurochemical Pathway

flowchart LR A(Heat) --> B(Serotonin depletion) B --> C(Irritability) C --> D(Loss of self-control) D --> E(Violence)

When the body gets too hot, it tries to cool down. One way it does this is by diverting resources away from other processes, dropping a brain chemical called serotonin. Serotonin is thought to help regulate mood and keep people calm. Without enough of it, people become much more aggressive and likely to snap — and beat up their wives.

Evidence: Moderate.

Issues: Laboratory and animal studies strongly support this link. However, it is difficult to isolate neurochemical changes as the sole cause of violence in real-world settings where social and economic factors also play a role.

Sources: Burke et al. (2015); Mulambo et al. (2025)

2. Economic Shock Pathway

flowchart LR A(Heat) --> B(Crop failure / low productivity) B --> C(Income loss) C --> D(Household stress) D --> E(Violence)

In many parts of the world, extreme heat ruins crops or makes it too hot for people to work effectively. This can lead to a sudden loss of money for a family. The extreme stress of not being able to afford food or bills creates a “pressure cooker” environment at home, where male partners may use violence to vent their frustration.

Also, men often have stereotypical roles as ‘providers’. When it becomes impossible to provide, some may feel they are losing their status or authority in the family. To compensate for this loss of status and power, men might lash out at their partners.

Evidence: Good.

Issues: Most evidence comes from developing countries and agrarian societies; the impact may be less direct in highly industrialised or air-conditioned economies, and also in countries where anti-DV norms are stronger. However, it is not clear whether the threats to men’s identity are a strong pathway.

Sources: Burke et al. (2015); Mulambo et al. (2025); Nguyen (2024)

2.2. Marrying Daughters Off Into Abusive Relationships

flowchart LR A(Heat / poverty) --> B(Resource scarcity) B --> C(Child marriage) C --> D(Abusive relationships)

“Lack of access to resources is a key driving factor for child marriage, with girls whose families’ incomes fall in the lowest quintile more than twice as likely to marry before the age of 18 than those whose families’ incomes are in the highest quintile.”

Sources: Pope et al. (2022); United States Department of State (2023)

3. Substance Use (Alcohol) Pathway

flowchart LR A(Heat) --> B(Physical discomfort) B --> C(Increased alcohol consumption) C --> D(Reduced inhibition) D --> E(Violence)

Hot weather causes physical discomfort and thirst, which often leads to an increase in alcohol consumption. Alcohol then lowers people’s ability to control their impulses; it acts as a “trigger” that makes someone who is already hot and angry more likely to become physically abusive.

Evidence: Moderate.

Issues: Data on alcohol involvement in domestic violence is often based on police suspicion and reports, so data is noisy, impacted by reporting biases, etc. Also, some studies found that the rise in violence happens regardless of whether the offender was sober or not.

Sources: Card & Dahl (2011); Mulambo et al. (2025); Nguyen (2024); Sanz-Barbero et al. (2018)

4. Changed Routines

flowchart LR A(Heat) --> B(Change in daily routine) B --> C(Increased time spent together) C --> D(Conflict opportunities) D --> E(Violence)

Extreme heat changes how people spend their day. It might force couples to stay inside small, poorly ventilated homes to avoid the sun. Spending more time together in uncomfortable, hot conditions simply provides more opportunities for arguments to start and escalate into physical fights.

Evidence: Moderate to Good.

Issues: It is hard to distinguish whether the heat itself causes the aggression, or if the violence is a result of people being in close proximity for longer periods.

Sources: Burke et al. (2015); Card & Dahl (2011); Mulambo et al. (2025)

5. Scarce Water and Dangerous Collection

flowchart LR A(Heat) --> B(Water and fuel scarcity) B --> C(Longer travel for resources) C --> D(Exposure to danger) D --> E(Violence)

Heatwaves can cause local water sources to dry up, and women and girls are forced to travel much longer distances to get water. This extra travel often takes them into unfamiliar or dangerous areas where they are at a much higher risk of being attacked.

Evidence: Moderate.

Issues: This pathway is primarily documented in regions suffering from instability or extreme poverty; this may not be an issue at all in high-income regions.

Sources: United States Department of State (2023)

6. Psychological Distress Pathway (GAAM)

flowchart LR A(Heat) --> B(Negative affect / pain) B --> C(Lower threshold for aggression) C --> D(Violence)

According to the General Affective Aggression Model (GAAM), the heat causes physical pain and being overheated can make people miserable. This negative feeling lowers people’s tolerance for other stressors, meaning they get angry much faster and over smaller things than they would if they were physically comfortable.

Evidence: Good.

Issues: The exact threshold temperature for violence appears to vary by region. People in hotter climates may be somewhat more used to high temperatures than those in cooler areas.

Sources: Burke et al. (2015); Mulambo et al. (2025); Sanz-Barbero et al. (2018)

7. Displacement and Social Support Erosion

flowchart LR A(Heat / disaster) --> B(Home destruction) B --> C(Relocation to shelters) C --> D(Loss of social networks) D --> E(Isolation) E --> F(Violence)

When extreme climate events or natural disasters destroy homes, families are often forced to move into temporary shelters or crowded settlements. Displacement cuts women off from their usual safety nets, like nearby relatives, friends, or trusted neighbours.

Without these social circles and while living in high-stress conditions with zero privacy, it becomes much harder for women to escape an abusive partner or get help, leading to a rise in domestic violence.

Evidence: Good.

Issues: It is often difficult to separate the violence caused specifically by the act of moving from the general psychological trauma and economic loss triggered by the disaster itself.

Sources: Pope et al. (2023)

Policy Approaches Resulting From This (Brainstorm for the Project)

  • Combat heat exposure and improve cooling access
  • Economic support for farmers and vulnerable households
  • Anti-child-marriage NGOs and financial support for families
  • Restrict hard alcohol access during extreme heat events
  • Create cooled, free third places for women
  • Support water infrastructure and water NGOs
  • Train shelter personnel to notice and respond to domestic violence
