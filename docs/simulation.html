Prediction Tool

Model:

As a reminder, here is the model that the following visualisation is based on:

Model 2

DVrct = β · Heatrct + γ · Rainrct + δc + τt + εrct
  • FE: Country + year
  • Controls: Rainfall, clustered SEs
  • Variation: Within-country, across regions
  • Question: Are hotter regions within a country more violent?

The coefficient for heat was 8.2 and significantly different from 0!

A 10 percentage-point increase in extreme-heat-month count is associated with a roughly 0.8 percentage-point increase in DV prevalence (controlling for rainfall, country, and year fixed effects etc. etc.).

An important concept to understand for this analysis is this:

A 0.8 percentage point increase seems small, but it is meaningful if you consider:
Even if the increase in risk for each person is tiny, multiplying that small increase over a large population can produce a huge number of additional cases overall.

Imagine: 1,000,000 women experience DV in a country
0.8% increase -> ~8,000 additional cases

Visualisation of The Effect

How this Plot Works:

The Plot is Based on Three Things

  • A statistical relationship between Heat(waves) and Domestic Violence, estimated based on the LivWell dataset
  • Differing Domestic Violence Prevalences in Different Countries
  • Three possible climate projections from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change Interactive Atlas dataset : a low emissions scenario, a medium, and a high emissions scenario

What Represents What?

  • Bubble SIZE represents DV prevalence
  • Bubble COLOUR represents heat
  • Lines represent expected levels of DV in the selected country across decades, in three different heating scenarios
  • The map always visualises the high warming scenario
  • The red outline marks the currently selected country

How Predicted DV is Calculated

Predicted DV for a country is based on baseline DV in that country (based on the LivWell dataset ) + the increase in heatwave exposure for a certain year × my coefficient.

Predicted DV = Baseline DV + (Increase in Heatwave Exposure × β)

Again:

A 10 percentage-point increase in extreme-heat-month count is associated with a roughly 0.8 percentage-point increase in DV prevalence.

The Three IPCC Heating Scenarios

The three climate scenarios come from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change Interactive Atlas dataset .

There are three scenarios:

  • a ~2.1°C increase in heat from 2015 until 2100
  • a ~5.6°C increase in heat until 2100
  • and a ~6.8°C increase in heat until 2100

The three lines visualise how DV is assumed to develop over the decades in the three different scenarios.

The lines seem close together, but don't let that fool you! Zoom into the plot to see how much they diverge.

And consider this very interesting statistical observation by Geoffrey Rose :

Small effects matter a lot when they affect large populations!

Look at the projections below! Different increases in climate correspond to thousands or even millions more women experiencing DV!

How the “Women Affected” Projections Work

The projections for women experiencing DV do this:

  • take predicted DV prevalence (based on the coefficient for effect of heat on DV x IPCC heat projection),
  • multiply it by female population (based on World Bank 2024 population estimates ),
  • and compute how many additional women could be affected.

Important Context About the Heat Measure

The climate projections are based on the IPCC variable:

tx35ba: "Bias adjusted monthly count of days with maximum near-surface temperature above 35°C."

However, my regression model used a somewhat different heat measure. The original analysis was based on extreme-heat months (2SD > average temp.), but the IPCC data measured days above 35°C.

35°C is not necessarily the same thing as "2 standard deviations above normal temperature". These are slightly different measures of heat.

Finally, a Disclaimer

This is a mere visualisation, not a scientific projection. My analysis was NOT perfectly controlled. Further, the calculations are very approximate, based on singular and differing data sources.

Several strong assumptions are being made.

  • The model assumes the same relationship continues: across decades, societies, adaptation levels, infrastructure, policy changes, migration, economic development... etc. etc.
  • Global heatwave exposure is used as an approximation for local exposure.
  • Country averages are used instead of detailed regional variation.
  • The binomial check did not support the effect strongly.

So in summary: This visualisation is illustrative rather than predictive.
