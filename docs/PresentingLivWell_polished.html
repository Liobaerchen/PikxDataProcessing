The model I wanted to go with before looking at the data was:

Model 1

\( \text{DV}_{rt} = \beta \cdot \text{Heat}_{rt} + \alpha_r + \gamma_t + \varepsilon_{rt} \)
  • Main variables: Heat and DV per region and year
  • Controls: subtract region fixed effects, (global) year fixed effects
  • Interpretation: compares a region to itself over time
  • Question: Does a region become more violent in hotter years compared to its baseline?
  • Variation used: within-region over time

Data Cleaning Details

Here you can see from which years I retained data, and also from which countries most data was available. DV availability was not great. Overall, I excluded five countries completely: BGD (Bangladesh); EGY (Egypt); GAB (Gabon); GHA (Ghana); BEN (Benin). Most because there was no DV data, but also some due to no variation in heat (probably only 1 year and one region was available).

Data Cleaning:

  • Started with: 1832 observations
  • Removed: 1074 rows (~~59% of all rows)
  • Removed BGD, EGY, GAB, GHA, BEN out of 39 countries
  • Not great for longitudinal ideas; most countries only have ~2 years available

Basically, DV is especially sparse

An Attempt to Account for This:

Model 2

\( \text{DV}_{rct} = \beta \cdot \text{Heat}_{rct} + \gamma \cdot \text{Rain}_{rct} + \delta_c + \tau_t + \varepsilon_{rct} \)
  • Main variables: Heat and DV in region within country and year
  • Controls: subtract country (NOT REGION) fixed effects, (global) year fixed effects, and added rainfall control
  • Meaning: compares different regions within the same country, within several years
  • Question: Are hotter regions within a country more violent, holding rainfall (and year) constant?
  • Variation used: within-country spatial variation. The model before was about time, this is using space more!

Essentially, this does not compare e.g. Armenia and Peru, which would get confounded by culture --> Remember Annika's paper! BUT it does not throw away so much data because it can just take individual regions in certain years as 'participants' and compare them, after controlling for culture, year, and rain).

To check the regional confounds in Model 2

Model 3

The more I read about past research about the impacts of heatwaves, the more authors I heard that critcised past studies and pointed out that cross-sectional data is confounded by cultural, regional, infrastructural, etc. differences and basically tells you nothing (e.g. Burke et al., 2015; Nguyen, 2024). So, I thought of the following strategy:

Nonparametric Verification Strategy

Idea:

  • Run Model 2 as the main analysis: country + year fixed effects, rainfall control, clustered standard errors.
  • As a nonparametric verification, run a binomial test:
  1. For each region, check if the hotter year is also the year with more violence
    • If I have two years, this is just a 1 or 0
    • If I have 3+, check all possible pairs and see if concordant pairs are more common than discordant. If yes, region is a 1. If no, region is a 0.
    • If equal, throw the region away (and report how many were dropped)
  2. Count the number of 1s out of the total number of regions
  3. Locate that in a Bernoulli distribution; that's the test

After running the binomial test, check whether the hotter year was also always the later year. If yes, acknowledge this as a limitation, but note that survey years are NOT always the same two years. There are different years available per country.

Also: Model 2 controls for year effects, which the binomial test cannot, so if both point in the same direction, that is reassuring.

I would have to throw 142 regions away for this, because they only have a single year. But I'd still have 185 regions and 594 observations.

Questions

  • If I do more exploratory questions with this dataset, theoretically, I thought this could be useful to inspire future research. However, if I use frequentist statistics, then the LivWell Dataset shouldn't be used after that, right? Because that would be HARKing at its finest. Should I do something like splitting the dataset and leaving parts of it unused?

  • The year control seems necessary, because it controls for global shocks that aren't heat related (like Covid, economy...). But is it an issue because this also controls for climate change?

  • I think controlling for country in my new model may be an issue. If I only have variation between regions within countries left, then I need to use that to figure out the impact of heat extremes. However, I think that if a country is hit by a heat extreme, that will affect almost all regions, depending on the size of that country. So, can I actually learn much by comparing regions within countries only? I understand that there is an effect of culture etc. between countries that could definitely confound DV differences, especially since I don't have many years available... what to do?

  • I am not controlling for region, because I wouldn't have variation left for my analysis. I think this may be defensible because regions are more similar than countries in culture etc. But would this be confounded too much by e.g. local wealth, whether they are farmers or city-people...?

  • After reading the paper Annika suggested, I am worried about bad control. I think I am not controlling for mediators (rain, year, or country-heat-baselines shouldn't mediate heat-shock -> SD). But I am a bit worried about having excluded wetter regions. Though I don't think it would be a collider and can't see other big issues with the selection. And, clearly, I select based on DV availability, which the LivWell people wrote is sparse and probably impacted by many factors (are women willing to make time and share such personal information)? What do you think?
